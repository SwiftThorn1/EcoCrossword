  1. Biome

    A biome is a region on Earth characterized by its climate as well as by the organisms that can be found in it.

    Aquatic biomes include:
    • lakes
    • wetlands
    • streams and rivers
    • estuaries
    Terrestrial biomes include:
    • tropical forest
    • desert
    • tundra
    • temperate grassland
     a terrestrial or aquatic life zone with unique defining characteristics
  2. Ecology

    Ecology is a broad field of study that deals with the complex interactions that occur between organisms and each other as well as with their environment. The scope of ecology can vary widely, from as narrow as individual organisms to all organisms on Earth.

    the study of interactions between organisms and the environment
  3. Ecotone

    Contrary to many maps, biomes don't have rigid, well-defined boundaries. Instead, there are ecotones, intermediate regions between biomes. These can be narrow or wide, gradual or abrupt.

    area of transition between biomes
  4. Microclimate

    Despite their name, microclimates aren't necessarily small. They refer to an area with different climate than the overall climate of the surroundings, or macroclimate. For example, it might be cooler near bodies of water than in the surrounding areas, or sunnier on one side of a mountain compared to the other.

    very fine local patterns of climate that may differ from larger scale climate
  5. Biotic

    Biotic factors are the organisms that make up an ecosystem. These include animals, plants, fungi, and bacteria.

    living environmental factors
  6. Dispersal

    Species do not stay in the same place forever. If they did, we would still be in Africa! This movement is called dispersal, and is limited by both biotic and abiotic factors that inhibits the species from surviving and reproducing in those areas.

    how far a species has spread from its origin

  1. Climate

    As opposed to weather, which can change daily, the climate of an area refers to the long-term conditions of an area. Key components of climate are temperature, precipitation, sunlight, and wind. Factors that may impact climate include ocean currents, altitude, and topography.

    long-term weather conditions of an area
  2. Abyssal zone

    The abyssal zone is one of the ocean, which is stratified into layers each with unique characteristics. Other zones are classified by light level, like the photic and aphotic zones, temperature, and community structure.

    deepest part of the ocean (2 words)
  3. Abiotic

    Abiotic factors make up what we mostly think of when we think of the environment and have a large impact on the species that can survive and reproduce in a particular area. These factors include soil, water, air, and salinity.

    nonliving environmental factors
  4. Climograph

    Climographs are a graphical way to observe differences in temperature and precipitation between different biomes, typically using a bar graph and a line graph to show each.

    a graph that shows the temperature and precipitation of a region

  1. Semelparity

    What do salmon and wheat have in common? Both are semelparous organisms, meaning they only reproduce once in their life cycles. This reproductive strategy is typically favoured in environments with low offspring survival rate and high mortality rate.

    "big-bang" reproductive strategy
  2. Density-dependent

    Populations cannot grow indefinitely, and this is due to density-dependent limiting factors that reduce birth rates and increase death rates as population density increases. Density-dependent limiting factors include competition for resources, disease, and predation.

    factors that limit population growth through negative feedback
  3. Life history

    An organism's life history shows the age of their first reproduction, how often they reproduce, and how many offspring are produced. These aspects of an organism's life can provide much insight on the environment they live in as well as natural selection in general.

    an organism's pattern of reproduction and survival
  4. Logistic

    The logistic growth model shows how population growth slows as populations approach their maximum size. It is usually a good model for small animals or microorganisms.

    growth model that levels off
  5. Metapopulation

    Metapopulations consist of "patches" of populations of the same species that may immigrate from and emigrate to other patches. If one patch goes extinct or immigrates, another population may inhabit it.

    group of populations of the same species that are linked in some way and that may interact
  6. Demography

    Demography involves the study of how populations change over time by looking at birth and death rates. To do this, demographers use various types of data structures, such as life tables, survivorship curves, and reproductive tables.

    the study of populations
  7. Carrying capacity

    A population's carrying capacity depends on the environment that the population is in and exists due to the limit on resources available. These essential resources include nutrients, water, and shelter. For humans, we are using many resources in nonsustainable ways, meaning we will inevitably reach the global carrying capacity.

    maximum sustainable population size (2 words)
  8. Iteroparity

    Iteroparity involves multiple iterations of reproductions, meaning many repetitions. This reproductive strategy is typically favoured in environments where the offspring are likely to survive and be able to reproduce. Also, iteroparous have multiple chances to reproduce if the time is inopportune.

    multiple offspring reproductive strategy
  9. Dispersion

    There are many ways in which individuals can be dispersed within a population. Clumped dispersion means the groups are clustered together, often due to uneven distribution of resources or for easier mating. Uniform dispersion means the individuals are evenly spaced out, usually due to social interactions such as territoriality. Finally, random dispersion occurs due to a lack of attraction or repulsion between individuals.

    pattern of spread among individuals in a population

Down

  1. Density

    Density is often difficult to calculate directly, as animals often move around and die, though researchers can use the mark-recapture method to estimate population sizes. On the other hand, the density of plant populations can be found relatively easily by taking samples of land and determining the number of individuals in each.

    number of individuals per area
  2. Population dynamics

    The field of population dynamics looks at how biotic and abiotic factors affect population sizes. It applies mathematics to ecology in order to understand how population sizes change.

    the study of factors on population size (2 words)
  3. Exponential

    The exponential growth model represents the maximum growth possible for a population under ideal conditions. It can be a useful model for new populations, which have little to no limiting factors on population size. The human population has undergone an exponential rise since 1650, but has since slowed down.

    growth model that shows maximum potential growth

  1. Adaptation

    Adaptations help organisms survive and reproduce. Many organisms have adaptations to avoid being eaten by other organisms, such as cryptic (camouflage) or aposematic (warning) coloration and mimicry.

    Likewise, predators often have adaptations, such as acute smell and hearing or sharp weapons like claws and teeth, that help them catch and kill prey.

    a beneficial trait that arises from natural selection and helps an organism be better suited to its environment
  2. Predation

    Predation involves any organism, a predator, eating another, the prey. This interaction is not limited to animals is essential to survive and reproduce, and thus many organisms have adaptations and strategies to accomplish it. Also, because it is so common, predation heavily encourages natural selection for the prey organisms.

    +/- interaction: one organism kills and eats another
  3. Community

    A community includes all the different populations in a certain area. This area can be small or large, and community ecology focuses on the different types of interactions that occur between the different species, or interspecific interactions, as well as the roles of each species within the community.

    a group of various populations of different species that interact with each other
  4. Succession

    Communities can change significantly over time, especially after events that cause massive change. These changes are called ecological succession. The first time the region is inhabited is called primary succession. Then, when disturbance occurs and clears the community, secondary succession occurs and new species may inhabit the area.

    a series of changes to the structure of a community: ecological __________
  5. Disturbance

    A disturbance in a community is a temporary event, such as a flood or drought, that permanently changes the conditions of the environment. This may sound like a bad thing, but most communities follow the nonequilibrium model, which states that communities are constantly changing rather than stable. Disturbance is actually an important part of community health, as the intermediate disturbance hypothesis states that species diversity is highest when there is moderate disturbance.

    event that greatly affects the environment in an area
  6. Pathogen

    Pathogens are disease-causing microorganisms. Despite their size, they can have a large impact on community structures by spreading diseases. Understanding their movement is essential to get a deeper insight into how diseases spread, especially as human activity spreads disease around the world at a faster rate than ever.

    a disease-causing organism
  7. Keystone

    Keystone species are species that may not be the most abundant, but have a disproportionately large impact on their community through their unique and essential niches. One example of a keystone species is the sea otter, which keep the kelp-eating sea urchin population in check. Without these otters, the sea urchin population would go out of control and kelp forests would die off.

    high-impact species on environment

  1. Latitudinal

    A tropical rainforest contains many more species than Antarctica does. This is called a latitudinal gradient, and is mostly due to evolutionary history and climate. The tropics are much older than polar communities, meaning more time for evolution and thus more species emerging. Also, the climate in the tropics are much more forgiving compared to the poles, allowing more species to survive and reproduce.

    the pattern of increasing species richness from the poles to the tropics: ___________ gradients
  2. Dominant

    Dominant species are the most common ones in a community. In BC forests, for example, two of the dominant species is the Western red cedar and the Douglas fir. Dominant species often have a competitive advantage over other species that allow them to survive and reproduce at a greater rate, which may give clues about the traits that are favoured in that environment.

    most abundant species
  3. Trophic

    Organisms in a community gather their energy and nutrients through various means. This organization is called the trophic structure, and is often represented in food chains or food webs to see how energy moves throughout the community. Each step in a food chain is a trophic level, with the lowest trophic level consisting of primary producers that make their own food.

    feeding relationships between organisms: _______ structure
  4. Herbivory

    A primary consumer eating parts of a plant or alga is an example of herbivory. Like predation, herbivory tends to encourage natural selection and leads to adaptations in both the herbivore, such as acute smell, special teeth or extra stomachs, as well as the plant, being eaten, like thorns or toxins.

    +/- interaction: an organism eats a plant or alga
  5. Symbiosis

    Many species live together for extended periods of their lives. This is called symbiosis. There are three types:

    1. parasitism - a parasite steals nutrients from their host (+/- interaction) e.g. lice living in hair
    2. mutualism - two species benefiting from each other (+/+ interaction) e.g. sea anemone protecting clownfish and clownfish scaring predator fish
    3. commensalism - one species benefits while other is unaffected (+/0 interaction) e.g. jackals feeding on remains of predators
    an intimate, long-term relationship between species
  6. Niche

    Just like how people each have a job to do to contribute to their communities, organisms have niches that defines their role in their ecological community. Niches may overlap due to competition, leading to a difference between fundamental niche (full potential) and realized niche (actual role). Having a niche ensures that competitive exclusion does not lead to the extinction of one species, and this difference in niches is called resource partitioning.

    a species' role in the ecosystem: ecological _____

  1. Nutrients

    Production in terrestrial and aquatic ecosystems is limited by nutrients, typically nitrogen and phosphorus. This is why it can be harmful to release too many of these nutrients into ecosystems, as it may cause excess plant growth.

    Another limiting factor of primary production in aquatic ecosystems is light, as sunlight cannot easily penetrate through water. However, the effect on light as a limiting factor is less than that of nutrients.

    main limiting factor of primary production in both terrestrial and aquatic ecosystems
  2. Trophic efficiency

    Energy is never 100% efficient, as it is lost through respiration and excreted by organisms. The actual energy gained by a higher trophic level is called trophic efficiency, and typically ranges from 5 to 20% with an average of 10%. This partly explains why there are so few apex predators, as the food consumption needed for the same amount of energy becomes massive.

    percentage of energy gained by one trophic level from consumption of a lower trophic level (2 words)
  3. Eutrophication

    When nutrients, usually nitrogen or phosphorus, are released into oceans or lakes, algae grow rapidly out of control, reducing oxygen levels and creating "dead zones" of low oxygen that cannot support aquatic life. This problem is exacerbated by the overuse of fertilizer and detergents that leach into bodies of water.

    abundance of nutrients leading to excess plant growth
  4. Biogeochemical

    Nutrients follow various different paths throughout the biosphere that involve biotic and abiotic components, so they are called biogeochemical cycles. Nutrients can be found in reservoirs, classified into organic and inorganic and whether the nutrients are directly available for use.

    pathway that nutrients follow: ______________ cycle
  5. Autotrophs

    Autotrophs, also known as primary producers, are needed to support all life in the ecosystem. They convert light energy to sugars to use as fuel. Autotrophs are generally plants, algae, or bacteria, which can then be consumed by heterotrophs to transfer energy throughout the ecosystem.

    organisms that use photosynthesis to produce sugars

  1. Detritivores

    Detritivores are like the recycling facilities of ecosystems. They feed on detritus, or dead organic material, such as remains of dead organisms and feces. Without them, organic matter would pile up on Earth and nutrients would run out. Not only that, but detritivores also convert organic compounds into inorganic compounds that primary producers need for growth.

    organisms who consume nonliving organic matter
  2. Ozone

    Ozone (O3) is a gas that protects the Earth from the Sun's dangerous UV radiation. However, the ozone layer has been depleting due to chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs), whose fluorine atoms combine with ozone to break it down. Though we have stopped using CFCs, the chlorine from the already existing CFCs are recycled in the atmosphere and are free to continue breaking down more ozone.

    a gas that forms a layer around the Earth blocks solar UV radiation
  3. Greenhouse effect

    Without greenhouse gases in the atmosphere, most solar radiation would reflect off the surface of the Earth and it would be too cold to live on. Greenhouse gases, such as carbon dioxide and methane, reflect the heat back to Earth and make it inhabitable. This process is called the greenhouse effect.

    However, in recent years, the burning of fossil fuels has released more CO2 into the atmosphere, causing a rise in global temperatures that can have dramatic effects all around the globe, including an increase in forest fires and the melting of Arctic ice that causes a rise in ocean levels.

    the process of gases reflecting solar energy back to the Earth (2 words)
  4. Acid rain

    Burning fossil fuels releases elements that react with water to form sulfuric or nitric compounds, which increases the acidity. Acid rain affects both aquatic and terrestrial ecosystems by lowering the pH in bodies of water to a level that can be damaging to fish and frogs. Acid rain also affects the soil, which can be dangerous for plants and can damage or destroy entire forests.

    precipitation with low pH level (2 words)
  5. Magnification

    Because organisms at a higher trophic levels consume much more biomass due to inefficiency of energy transfer, they also ingest more toxins. This process, called biological magnification, gets compounded with increasing trophic levels. This is why the consumption of too many apex predators can be dangerous for humans.

    the process of toxins accumulating at increasing trophic levels: biological _____________

  1. Endangered

    An endangered species is one that is likely to soon become extinct, either in a particular region or completely, especially if no conservation efforts are taken to prevent it. There are over 40,000 endangered species all around the world.

    species that is in danger of extinction
  2. Services

    Ecosystems provide us with countless benefits, which are called ecosystem services. Just to name a few, ecosystems grant us food, land, and other resources, purify the air that we breathe and water that we drink, and decompose the waste that we release into the environment, all at no cost to us.

    ways in which ecosystems help humans: ecosystem ________
  3. Extinction vortex

    An already small population has the potential to keep shrinking due to an effect called an extinction vortex. For example, a small population may have reduced variability, leading it more prone to disease that kills off more the population and so on, creating a vicious cycle of positive feedback that may drive the population to extinction.

    positive feedback loop that leads to a population shrinking (2 words)
  4. Habitat loss

    Habitat loss due to agriculture, forestry, and pollution have been the biggest factor in the reduction in biodiversity and has driven many species to extinction. Habitat loss has had a profound effect on the population health of both terrestrial species, such giant pandas and tigers, as well as aquatic ones, such as marine turtles and whales.

    Two other threats to biodiversity are overexploitation, which includes overfishing and poaching, and introduced species, which can either be accidental or intentional.

    greatest threat to biodiversity (2 words)

  1. Threatened

    Threatened species are ones that will soon become endangered. Here in Canada, some examples include the black-footed ferret, the peary caribou, and the Vancouver Island marmot.

    species that will soon be in danger of extinction
  2. Bioremediation

    Bioremediation is a process used in restoration ecology that uses organisms, typically microorganisms, to break down pollutants and detoxify ecosystems. These microorganisms use enzymes to convert toxic substances into less harmful compounds. This process is often used to clean up oil spills.

    use of organisms to detoxify ecosystems
  3. Augmentation

    Biological augmentation adds organisms to an ecosystem to restore needed nutrients. The organism used varies depending on the ecosystem, but they are often nitrogen-fixing microorganisms that are essential for plant growth.

    use of organisms to add nutrients to ecosystems: biological ____________
  4. Movement corridor

    Movement corridors are strips of habitat that connect two regions together, serving as bridges for animals. Movement corridors can prevent genetic drift and inbreeding, even when the habitat has been fragmented. However, they can also promote disease by allowing travel. Nevertheless, movement corridors are an essential part of minimizing the negative effects of habitat fragmentation.

    area that connects fragmented habitats (2 words)
  5. Zoned reserve

    Zoned reserves are used to maintain the economic value of the land while also protecting threatened and endangered species. The surrounding area also serves as a buffer zone between humans and wildlife.

    protected area surrounded by economically useful land (2 words)
  6. Hot spot

    Biodiversity hot spots contain many species that cannot be found anywhere else on the planet, meaning it is particularly critical for us to preserve these regions of high biodiversity. Maintaining healthy ecosystems in these areas will drastically help global biodiversity.

    area with disproportionately high number of endemic species: biodiversity ___ ____ (2 words)
